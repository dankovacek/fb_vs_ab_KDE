Technical note: an empirically grounded kernel bandwidth function for streamflow density estimation

When does bandwidth choice matter for flow duration curves?

{{ metadata.date }}, working note

Abstract

  • Fixed bandwidth KDE with a global scale estimator minimizes MISE under a Gaussian reference. The embedded normal-reference approximation \(\int [f''(x)]^2\,dx\) is poor for multimodal densities. The resulting homoscedastic bandwidth is inconsistent with heteroscedastic streamflow measurement uncertainty even after log-transform.
  • We present an adaptive bandwidth grounded in an approximated hydrometric error model, with a data-spacing floor in log space that keeps each bandwidth from falling below the local observation gap. Compared against Silverman’s rule for {{ metadata.n_stations }} stations across {{ metadata.regions }}.
  • Silverman’s rule gives MISE within 10 % of optimal for log-space skewness \(|s| \le 1.8\) and mode separation \(\le 3\sigma\). Mode separation: 0 % of stations exceed \(3\sigma\). Skewness: 3 to 16 % of stations exceed \(|s|=1.8\) across regions. For the 84 to 97 % within the bound, AB and FB are structurally indistinguishable by all five divergence metrics. For the 3 to 16 % outside it, AB applies locally-adaptive smoothing calibrated to measurement uncertainty and observation density. Its OOB bootstrap score is also less sensitive to year composition than FB at these same stations.

2Problem statement

  • Daily streamflow is expressed as unit-area runoff (UAR = Q/A, Q in m3 s−1, A in km2, units L s−1 km−2). Silverman’s rule derives a single global bandwidth by minimizing MISE under a Gaussian reference, using the normal distribution to evaluate the unknown roughness integral \(\int [f''(x)]^2\,dx\). This overestimates the optimal bandwidth in flat regions (small \(|f''|\)) and underestimates it at modes and regime transitions (large \(|f''|\)). Streamflow uncertainty is also heteroscedastic: relative error is highest at low flows (extrapolated ratings, ice) and at extreme high flows (out-of-bank conditions), yet the global bandwidth treats all flow magnitudes equivalently.
  • The adaptive estimator sets a per-observation floor \(h_i = \max\!\bigl(h_\text{Voronoi},\;\ln(1+\varepsilon(q_i))\bigr)\), where \(\varepsilon(q)\) is the piecewise fractional error at flow \(q\) (Figure 1) and \(h_\text{Voronoi}\) is the Voronoi cell half-width in log-UAR space. Each bandwidth reflects the local observation gap, not the global scale. The log transform widens gaps in the sparse low-flow tail, and the Voronoi floor widens with them. Caveat: very few low-flow points yield broad, near-uninformative kernels there, so this is not obviously better than Silverman.
  • This study compares the two estimators across {{ metadata.n_stations }} stations ({{ metadata.regions }}) at grid resolutions of 6, 8, and 10 bits to characterise the severity and frequency of divergence across flow regimes, and to identify whether catchment and climate characteristics can predict large divergence.

4Methodology

Notation. \(X\): UAR observation. \(f(x)\): PDF of \(X\) in log-UAR space. \(F(x) = P(X \le x)\): CDF. \(F^{-1}(p)\): quantile function (inverse CDF). Subscripts AB / FB: adaptive bandwidth (AB) and fixed (Silverman rule of thumb) bandwidth (FB) respectively.

KDE in log-UAR space = Gaussian kernel smoothing of log-transformed observations. For \(X \sim \mathrm{LN}(\mu,\sigma)\), \(\ln X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma)\).

Silverman’s rule (MISE-optimal under Gaussian reference, Silverman 1986 Ch. 3): $$h_\text{FB} = 0.9 \min\!\left(\hat{\sigma},\,\frac{\text{IQR}}{1.34}\right) n^{-1/5}$$ \(\hat{\sigma}\): sample std dev of log observations. \(\min\): Scott’s (1992) heavy-tail robustification.

Adaptive estimator: \(h_i = \max(h_\text{Voronoi},\;\ln(1+\varepsilon(q_i)))\). Grid: log-UAR, \([\text{UAR}_\text{min},\,\text{UAR}_\text{max}]\), \(2^b\) bins, \(b \in \{6,8,10\}\). Five divergence measures per station per bitrate:

  • MISE (mean integrated squared error): integrated squared difference between the two KDE density estimates in log-UAR space (log-flow units⁻¹). $$\mathrm{MISE} = \int \!\left(f_\mathrm{AB}(x) - f_\mathrm{FB}(x)\right)^2 d(\ln x) = \sum_j \frac{\left(p_\mathrm{AB}(x_j) - p_\mathrm{FB}(x_j)\right)^2}{\delta_j}$$ where \(\delta_j\) converts probability mass to density. Silverman’s rule minimises MISE under a Gaussian reference. Unlike ED², MISE penalises pointwise density disagreement rather than CDF-gap area, so it is more sensitive to narrow peaks where the two estimators assign very different heights.
  • KS statistic: the maximum absolute CDF difference over all grid points \(x_j\). $$D_\text{KS} = \max_j \left| F_\text{AB}(x_j) - F_\text{FB}(x_j) \right|, \quad F(x_j) = \sum_{k \le j} p(x_k)$$
  • Implied quantile shift: a practitioner-interpretable translation of any CDF gap into a relative flow change. At bin \(j\) with log-UAR width \(\delta_j\) and probability mass \(p_j\), the local density is \(\hat{f}(x_j) = p_j/\delta_j\). If the two CDFs differ by \(\varepsilon\) at \(x_j\), the first-order approximation (\(\hat{f}\) locally constant) gives the log-space displacement needed to accumulate probability \(\varepsilon\): $$\hat{f}(x_j)\,\Delta(\ln x) \approx \varepsilon \;\Longrightarrow\; \Delta(\ln x) = \varepsilon\,\frac{\delta_j}{p_j}$$ Converting to a relative flow change: $$\text{shift\%} = \left(e^{\,\varepsilon\,\delta_j/p_j} - 1\right) \times 100$$ Setting \(\varepsilon = D_\text{KS}\) and \(j = j^*\) (the KS-maximum bin) gives the scalar reported in section 6.4.6. Because \(j^*\) maximises \(\varepsilon\) rather than the ratio \(\varepsilon/p_j\), a bin with low density and only a marginally smaller CDF gap can yield a substantially larger implied shift. The KS-point shift is therefore a lower bound on the worst-case shift across the support; section 6.4.7 presents the full-support sweep.
  • Wasserstein-1 distance (W₁): the total area between the two CDFs measured in log-UAR space (log-flow units). $$W_1 = \sum_j \left| F_\text{AB}(x_j) - F_\text{FB}(x_j) \right| \delta_j$$ where \(\delta_j\) is the log-UAR bin width at grid point \(j\). Displayed in % as \(100 \times (\mathrm{e}^{W_1} - 1)\): a value of P% means the observed CDF-gap area equals that produced by scaling all flow values up by P%.
  • Energy distance squared (ED²): the squared L² area between the two CDFs in log-UAR space (log-flow units), on the same scale as W₁. $$\mathrm{ED}^2 = 2\sum_j \left( F_\text{AB}(x_j) - F_\text{FB}(x_j) \right)^2 \delta_j$$ Penalises large sustained CDF deviations more than W₁ does, because squaring the gap amplifies peaks while leaving small gaps nearly unchanged.
  • KL divergence (KL): bits of information lost when the Silverman PMF is used to approximate the adaptive PMF. AB is treated as the reference distribution; FB is the approximation under scrutiny. $$D_\text{KL}(\text{AB}\,\|\,\text{FB}) = \sum_j p_\text{AB}(x_j) \log_2 \frac{p_\text{AB}(x_j)}{p_\text{FB}(x_j)}$$ A small uniform mixture (\(\delta\)-constrained, \(\delta =\) KLD_DELTA_MAX bits) is applied to both PMFs before evaluation to handle zero bins in either estimator; this floor acts as a precision floor on the probability assigned to any state, and the resulting KL measures the penalty incurred by FB for underspecifying the probability of states where AB concentrates mass. This framing is appropriate when Silverman’s conditions are violated: FB over-smooths peaks and troughs that AB resolves, and the KL correctly flags the information cost of that under-specification. Where Silverman’s conditions are satisfied, the interpretation is reversed: AB may be over-fitting sampling variability at the scale of individual bins, and the FB smoothing is consistent with MISE-minimisation under the Gaussian reference. In those cases the same \(D_\text{KL}(\text{AB}\,\|\,\text{FB})\) value conflates genuine FB failure with AB roughness, so KL should be read alongside the stratification results in section 6.4. High KL with modest KS/W₁ indicates the two CDFs are globally close but the PDFs disagree sharply at narrow peaks or inter-modal troughs.

5Synthetic validation

All scenarios use \(N = 3650\) samples (10 yr × 365 d) drawn from a finite log-normal mixture \(f(x) = \sum_k w_k \cdot \mathrm{LN}(\mu_k,\sigma_k)\) with fully specified parameters. All five divergence metrics are computed against \(f_\text{true}\) directly (AB vs truth and FB vs truth), and \(D(\text{AB},\text{FB})\) is computed between the two estimators so that agreement and disagreement can be distinguished from accuracy.

5.1 Baseline scenarios

Two controlled distributions establish the behaviour of AB and FB where the ground truth is known exactly.

  • Scenario 1 (asymmetric bimodal). \(w_1=0.35\), \(\mu_1=\ln(0.3)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\), \(\sigma_1=0.20\); \(w_2=0.65\), \(\mu_2=\ln(30)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\), \(\sigma_2=0.90\). No measurement artifacts. Tests bimodal recovery with a 4.5× spread contrast between components.
  • Scenario 2 (rating-curve precision floor). \(w_1=0.25\), \(\mu_1=\ln(0.02)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\), \(\sigma_1=0.30\) (low-Q pool inside the high-error zone); \(w_2=0.75\), \(\mu_2=\ln(0.5)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\), \(\sigma_2=0.10\) (narrow high-flow mode). \(Q < 0.1\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\) is quantized to the nearest \(0.01\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\) step (rating-curve resolution floor). The narrow mode forces a small Silverman \(h\), which then preserves quantization spikes in the low-Q tail; the adaptive floor smooths them. Orange shading marks the high-error zone (\(Q < 0.1\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\)).

5.2 Mode-separation sweep

Component spreads (\(\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = 0.55\)) and weights (\(w_1 = w_2 = 0.5\)) are held constant; only the log-space separation \(\Delta\mu = \mu_2 - \mu_1\) is varied from 0 to 5 in six equal steps. At \(\Delta\mu = 0\) the two components coincide, collapsing to a single log-normal where AB and FB are expected to agree (lower bound). As \(\Delta\mu\) grows the distribution becomes increasingly bimodal and Silverman’s bandwidth is stretched across the widening inter-modal gap, over-smoothing both modes simultaneously until the error approaches an upper plateau.

5.3 Spread-asymmetry sweep

Silverman (1986) addresses skewness and bimodal separation as the two conditions that bound the MISE optimality of his rule of thumb. This sweep identifies a third condition: asymmetry in the spread of the two components. When one component is substantially broader than the other, the fixed bandwidth is attracted to the narrower mode and over-smooths the broader tail, even when the modes are not strongly separated.

Component means (\(\mu_1 = \ln(2)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\), \(\mu_2 = \ln(8)\,\mathrm{m^3\,s^{-1}}\); separation \(\approx 2.5\sigma\)) and weights (\(w_1 = w_2 = 0.5\)) are fixed so that at ratio 1:1 Silverman performs reasonably and the asymmetry failure mode emerges cleanly as the ratio grows. The ratio \(\sigma_1 / \sigma_2\) is swept from 1:1 (symmetric spread) to 12:1 on a log scale in six steps, with the harmonic mean of \((\sigma_1, \sigma_2)\) held constant so that total mixture spread is comparable across panels. Extending the sweep to 12:1 allows the score curves to approach their upper plateau.

5.4 MISE and ED are not interchangeable

MISE and ED integrate over different quantities: $$\text{MISE} = \sum_j \frac{(\hat{f}_j - f_j)^2}{\delta_j}, \qquad \text{ED}^2 = 2\sum_j (\hat{F}_j - F_j)^2\,\delta_j$$ MISE sums squared PMF residuals \((\hat{f} - f)\). ED sums squared CDF gaps \((\hat{F} - F)\). Because a local density error accumulates in the CDF but a distant error of equal magnitude does not, the two metrics can rank estimators in opposite order.

Each column below uses the Scenario 2 bimodal distribution (low-Q pool plus narrow high-Q peak, the same true PMF as section 5.1) as the reference. The top panel overlays two perturbed estimators on the true distribution. The middle panel shows the PMF residual \((\hat{f} - f)\), the pointwise integrand of MISE. The bottom panel shows the CDF gap \((\hat{F} - F)\), the pointwise integrand of ED. The score table above the figures reports MISE and ED² for each estimator against the true distribution.

Example 1 holds MISE constant while varying ED. Both A and B remove mass ε from the narrow high-Q peak. A deposits it at the adjacent bin. B deposits it at the low-Q pool, about 40 bins away. The PMF residuals are equal in magnitude so MISE is unchanged. The CDF gap for B persists across the full inter-modal distance, making ED about 6.3× larger.

Example 2 holds ED approximately constant while varying MISE. Both C and D move mass M from the narrow peak to the low-Q pool, so the CDF gap profiles are nearly identical (ED within about 4 %). C deposits the mass at a single bin (spike). D spreads the same mass over five adjacent bins. MISE penalises C’s height concentration 5× more than D’s spread.

6.1 Silverman theoretical validity bounds

Silverman (1986, §3.4) guarantees that the rule-of-thumb bandwidth gives MISE within 10 % of optimal for log-space skewness \(|s| \le 1.8\) and mode separation \(\le 3\sigma\). Mode separation is not a binding constraint: 0 % of stations across all regions exceed the \(3\sigma\) threshold. Skewness is binding for a minority: 3 to 16 % of stations exceed \(|s|=1.8\) depending on region. 6.4 Stratified divergence by Silverman validity conditions

Four complementary stratification schemes are shown. Rule Set A (section 6.4.1) uses Silverman’s documented conditions: log-space skewness and bimodal separation. Rule Set B (section 6.4.2) uses an empirically identified third condition not covered by Silverman (1986): asymmetry in the spread of the two mixture components. When one component is much broader than the other, the fixed bandwidth is attracted to the narrower mode and over-smooths the broader tail. This condition is quantified as \(\max(\sigma_\text{left}, \sigma_\text{right}) / \min(\sigma_\text{left}, \sigma_\text{right})\) and is evaluated only for bimodal stations (n_peaks ≥ 2; dip test p < 0.05 where available). Rule Set D (section 6.4.5) combines all five criteria simultaneously.

6.4.2 Rule Set B: divergence by spread asymmetry (bimodal stations only)

Bimodal stations are identified using two independent criteria applied in sequence. (1) KDE peak structure: the AB KDE PMF is searched for peaks with prominence ≥ 0.005 (catching modes with as little as ~2 % of total PMF mass) using scipy.signal.find_peaks. 6.4.3 Mass concentration into too few bins

An artifact not represented in the synthetic sweep arises when a station’s observed flow record produces a PMF where the bulk of the total probability mass falls into just a handful of grid cells. This happens in discretely quantized records (e.g. HYSETS stations whose daily flow values repeat at a small set of rounded levels) and in very short records where the empirical distribution is nearly concentrated on one or two flow magnitudes. When {{ bin_conc_n_bins }} or fewer bins hold a large fraction of the total mass, the KDE is effectively being asked to smooth a near-Dirac comb, and the Silverman bandwidth (derived from a Gaussian reference) is poorly suited to that task.

6.4.8 Large ISD within Silverman bounds

The three stations below have the largest \(\mathrm{ISD}(\hat{f}_\mathrm{AB},\hat{f}_\mathrm{FB})\) among all stations that pass both Silverman validity criteria: \(|\gamma_1| < {{ skew_threshold | default('1.8') }}\) (log-space skewness) and mode separation \(\Delta\mu \le {{ peak_sep_threshold | default('3') }}\,\sigma\) (or unimodal). Satisfying these conditions gives Silverman’s strongest theoretical assurance that \(\hat{f}_\mathrm{FB}\) will be near-optimal relative to the true density. That assurance, however, concerns optimality against truth, not agreement between the two estimators. Even with all criteria satisfied, \(\hat{f}_\mathrm{AB}\) and \(\hat{f}_\mathrm{FB}\) can still assign materially different probability mass to specific flow ranges. Annotations show \(\gamma_1\) (skewness) and \(\Delta\mu\) (peak separation, or “unimodal”) with a ✓ PASS badge confirming that the criterion is satisfied. The practical implication is that passing Silverman’s criteria does not allow a practitioner to infer that the choice of bandwidth rule is inconsequential.

8Discussion checklist

  • KS increases with bitrate? → estimators disagree at finer scales, coarser grids mask it.
  • Worst-KS stations perennial or ephemeral? Ephemeral: many zero-flow days clipped to min UAR; both CDFs concentrated near lower bound, may amplify or suppress differences.
  • Sharper adaptive peaks where observations repeat? Frozen-flow: many identical daily values. Voronoi floor widens kernels there; Silverman smooths globally, may spread mass away from true cluster.
  • Silverman systematically over-smoothed in low-flow tail? PDFs disagreeing mainly at low UAR → Silverman over-spreading mass below the measurement uncertainty floor.
  • Divergence decreases with record length? Longer records shrink Voronoi half-widths, pulling adaptive bandwidths toward Silverman in dense regions.
  • CDFs agree in bulk, diverge only at tails? Tail divergence matters most for flood/drought quantile estimation where small CDF gaps = large return-level shifts.
  • KS max in high \(|f''(x)|\) regions? Silverman most wrong where curvature deviates from Gaussian reference. Compute \(|\hat{f}''(x_{j^*})|\) at max-KS bin to test.
  • High-KL/low-KS: CDFs close globally, PDFs disagree sharply at localized bins (sparse tail, inter-modal trough where Silverman spreads mass away). High-KS/low-KL: broad CDF shift, no sharp PMF redistribution. Different diagnostic paths.
  • Define severity threshold (e.g. KS > 0.05 or quantile shift > 10%); report fraction of stations exceeding it per region.

Note: divergence metrics and the integral duality

\(\Delta(x)=F_P(x)-F_Q(x)\): pointwise CDF gap. \(Q^{-1}(p)-P^{-1}(p)\): quantile-space counterpart. Both are dual slices of the same enclosed area in the \((x,p)\) plane.

  • L1: \(\int_x|\Delta(x)|\,dx = \int_0^1|Q^{-1}(p)-P^{-1}(p)|\,dp = W_1\) (identical by duality).
  • L2: not equal. Quantile gap \(\text{gap}(p)=\Delta(x)/f(x)\), so $$\frac{\text{gap}(p)^2\,dp}{\Delta(x)^2\,dx}=\frac{1}{f(x)}.$$ Equal only when \(f(x)=1\) everywhere (uniform).
  • Log-UAR space: reduces variation in \(1/f(x)\) vs raw flow space. For log-normal stations ED ≈ W₂ rank order. For multimodal/heavy-tailed log-space distributions they can disagree in both magnitude and rank, in the same regime where fixed-bandwidth KDE is most likely to mis-represent the density.
Name Formula Space
W₁ (Wasserstein-1, MAE) \(\int|\Delta(x)|\,dx\) CDF ⇔ quantile
Cramér distance \(\int\Delta(x)^2\,dx\) CDF
Energy distance (ED) \(2\int\Delta(x)^2\,dx\) CDF (= Cramér × 2)
W₂ (Wasserstein-2, RMSE) \(\int(Q^{-1}(p)-P^{-1}(p))^2\,dp\) quantile

Consequence for large heterogeneous catchment samples.

  • Raw x-space: weights large-flow events heavily (x-axis dominated by high magnitudes).
  • Log-UAR space (used here): equal weight per decade, approximating L1 without explicit normalisation.
  • Quantile space (W₁/W₂): p-axis on [0,1] removes scale problem, but shifts weight to low-flow end for ephemeral catchments where measurement uncertainty is highest.
  • Volume-normalised MAE: corrects for catchment size, but inflates penalty for dry catchments near detection floor.
  • No single normalisation is unbiased across all hydrological regimes. The choice depends on which part of the distribution matters for the application.
